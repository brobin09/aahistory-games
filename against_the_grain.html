The Historian's Casebook
Four cold cases. Eleven documents. Every source in this archive is arguing with you — the advertisement has interests, the petition has fears, the memoir has eighty-eight years of distance. Your job is not to find the source that tells the truth. It is to interrogate each one, connect what they let slip, and make the sources tell on each other. Leads are limited. Spend them where the record is hiding something.
READ · INTERROGATE · CONNECT · VERDICT
Documents in Cases I, II, and IV are composites — invented texts built faithfully from the patterns of real archival records, so noted on each. Case III condenses the published accounts of two famous visitors, abridged for play and so labeled.

A NOTE FROM THE ARCHIVIST

THE METHOD

READ
Study every document tab. Blacked-out passages are unexamined — the right interrogation opens them. Notice what each document says, and what it refuses to say.
INTERROGATE — leads are limited
Every question costs one lead, and there are more questions than leads. Good questions secure evidence cards and open sealed passages. Dead ends teach a source's limits — and cost the same.
CONNECT — the evidence board
Tap two cards to tie a thread. True connections become new evidence — corroborations and contradictions — and some findings can only be carried by connection evidence. False threads weaken the case by 2.
VERDICT — claim and citation
Each finding needs the right claim and the right evidence cited beneath it. A true claim with a weak citation only half holds. The verdict, once stamped, closes the attempt.
GRADES
AIRTIGHT · STRONG · CIRCUMSTANTIAL · DISMISSED. AIRTIGHT demands nearly every shred in the box. Two verdicts at STRONG or better break the seal on Box IV. Reopen any case to argue it better.
