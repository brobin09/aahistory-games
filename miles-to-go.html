The Traveler's Almanac

📗 THE GREEN BOOK

From 1936 to 1966, Victor Hugo Green — a Black postal carrier from Harlem — published an annual guide listing hotels, gas stations, restaurants, tourist homes, and beauty shops across America that would serve Black travelers. It grew from a local pamphlet to a national institution selling tens of thousands of copies, sold at Esso stations and by mail. Families planned entire routes around its pages.

🌇 SUNDOWN TOWNS

Thousands of American towns — most of them outside the South — excluded Black people after dark, by ordinance, by posted signs, or by unwritten rule enforced with harassment and violence. Historian James Loewen documented the practice across the country and found it far more widespread than most Americans ever learned. For a Black family, nightfall between cities was not an inconvenience; it was a hazard to be engineered around, every single day of a trip.

🏠 TOURIST HOMES

Since most hotels and motels refused Black guests, private families opened their homes as "tourist homes" — a spare room, a hot breakfast, and safety, listed in the guide by the owner's name. They were the backbone of Black travel, an economy of hospitality built where the public one refused to function.

🧺 THE PICNIC BASKET

Black families packed fried chicken, boiled eggs, pound cake, and thermoses of coffee not as a picnic tradition but as infrastructure — food for the hundreds of miles where no kitchen would serve them. The shoebox lunch is remembered warmly, but it was born of necessity. Shaded pull-offs and church groves, passed along by word of mouth and penciled into the guide's margins, served as the dining rooms of the road.

🍑 ROADSIDE STANDS

Farm stands sold to anyone with a nickel — a cash transaction at a plank table asked no questions a lunch counter did. Travelers' memoirs remember the melon from the washtub of ice and the paper sack of peaches as small mercies of the road, and Black farm families running stands were themselves part of the route's quiet economy.

⛽ ESSO STATIONS

Esso (later Exxon) stood out among oil companies for serving Black motorists and franchising stations to Black owners — one reason Esso stations appear so often in the guide's pages, and why the guide itself was sold at many of them.

📻 THE RADIO DIAL

In 1948 Memphis's WDIA became the first American station programmed entirely for Black listeners — 50,000 watts of blues, gospel, and news, with B.B. King spinning records before his own career took off. Chess Records was cutting Muddy Waters in Chicago; New Orleans had its own "sepia stations." The dial mapped Black America as surely as the guide did, and a family could hear the region change out the window.

💵 MONEY ON THE ROAD

Travel ran on cash — credit cards were rare and bank access away from home rarer, so a family carried its whole trip in the glovebox and a hidden reserve in a coffee can. A padded "fine" at a speed trap or an overpriced repair came straight out of the vacation, which is why travelers drove gently, carried spare parts, and did their own roadside repairs when they could.

🚔 SPEED TRAPS & THE ROADSIDE STOP

Small-town speed traps squeezed money from all travelers, but a traffic stop carried different stakes for a Black driver — searches, invented fines, escalation with no recourse. Families drove rigidly under the limit through hostile counties and taught their children the survival etiquette of the roadside stop.

⚖️ AFTER 1964

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 outlawed discrimination in public accommodations, and the guide ceased publication in 1966 — the outcome its founder had explicitly hoped for. The last editions note the change in tone: a guide preparing for its own obsolescence, which was the whole point.
